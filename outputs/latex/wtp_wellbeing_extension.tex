\begin{titlepage}
\begin{center}
\vspace*{1.5cm}
{\large \textbf{Does Baseline Willingness-to-Pay Predict\\[0.3em]
Life Satisfaction Gains from Electrification?\\[0.3em]
Evidence from Rural Kenya}}\\[2em]
{\normalsize Extension of Lee, Miguel \& Wolfram (JPE 2019)}\\[1.5em]
{\normalsize \today}
\end{center}

\vspace{2em}
\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We ask whether a household's stated willingness-to-pay (WTP) for a grid
electricity connection at baseline predicts the life satisfaction gains it
realises from connection. Using data from the randomised experiment of
Lee, Miguel \& Wolfram (2019), we merge a contingent-valuation WTP measure
from the 2014 baseline survey with subjective wellbeing outcomes from two
follow-up rounds (2016, 2017). We estimate heterogeneous intent-to-treat
(ITT) effects on a normalised life satisfaction index and a
perceptions-of-security index, interacting the treatment indicator with a
binary high-WTP variable. The differential ITT coefficient is small
(${-}0.018$ standard deviations) and statistically insignificant in all
specifications. After Benjamini--Hochberg correction, neither outcome
survives multiple-testing adjustment ($q \geq 0.95$). These results are
consistent with the null average wellbeing effects reported by the original
paper and suggest that prior expectations, as proxied by stated WTP, do not
meaningfully moderate the wellbeing impact of grid connection.
\end{abstract}
\end{titlepage}

% ── 1. Background and Motivation ─────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Background and Motivation}
\label{sec:background}

A growing literature examines the welfare effects of household
electrification in developing countries. Lee, Miguel \& Wolfram (2019)
conduct a randomised experiment in rural Kenya in which households are
randomly offered grid connections at varying subsidy levels. Despite large
take-up responses to price subsidies, the authors find small or null
average effects on income, employment, and subjective wellbeing.

One mechanism that could generate heterogeneous wellbeing effects even
within an experiment with null average effects is an \emph{expectations
channel}: households that valued the connection more highly ex ante---as
revealed by higher stated WTP---may have had stronger prior expectations
of benefit. If electrification meets those expectations, high-WTP
households should exhibit larger life satisfaction gains than low-WTP
households; if it fails to meet them, the reverse could hold.

This note tests the expectations channel directly by interacting the
experimental treatment indicator with a baseline WTP measure. The
specification follows Lee, Miguel \& Wolfram (2019, JEP) Appendix
Tables A3--A4, which interact grid connection with a high-SES composite
index. We substitute the SES index with a binary high-WTP indicator
derived from the contingent-valuation exercise conducted at baseline.

% ── 2. Data and Specification ────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Data and Specification}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection*{Data}

We use three datasets from Lee, Miguel \& Wolfram (2019). The baseline
survey (\texttt{demand.dta}, 2014) contains the WTP elicitation and
treatment assignment. Two follow-up surveys---Round~1
(\texttt{impacts\_1.dta}, 2016) and Round~2 (\texttt{impacts\_2.dta},
2017)---contain the outcome variables. All three share a unique household
identifier (\texttt{reppid}), enabling a 1:1 merge. We drop households
already connected at baseline (\texttt{base\_connected}$=1$) and restrict
to adult respondents (\texttt{child}$=0$), following the original papers.

\subsection*{WTP Measure}

At baseline, each household was randomly assigned a hypothetical connection
price (\texttt{WTP\_amt} $\in \{0,\,10{,}000,\,\ldots,\,75{,}000\}$~KES)
and asked whether it would accept (\texttt{WTP\_r1}$\in\{0,1\}$). We
define stated WTP as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:wtp}
\text{WTP-stated}_i \;=\; \texttt{WTP\_amt}_i
  \;\times\; \mathbf{1}[\texttt{WTP\_r1}_i = 1].
\end{equation}
Since the elicitation price is randomly assigned, stated WTP has an
exogenous component. The sample median of WTP-stated equals zero (56.4\,\%
of households declined the offer), so
$\texttt{wtp\_high}_i = 1$ effectively identifies the 43.6\,\% of
households that accepted at any positive price.

\subsection*{Outcomes}

Our primary outcome is \texttt{life\_index}, a normalised life satisfaction
index available in both R1 and R2. Our secondary---and exploratory---outcome
is \texttt{r\_crime\_index}, the perceptions-of-security index (JPE
Table~3, row~D6), available in R2 only. Both indices are standardised to
mean zero and unit standard deviation in the control group.

\subsection*{Specification}

We estimate:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:spec}
y_{ic} \;=\; \alpha
  \;+\; b_1\,\text{Treat}_{ic}
  \;+\; b_2\,\text{HighWTP}_{i}
  \;+\; b_3\,(\text{Treat}_{ic} \times \text{HighWTP}_{i})
  \;+\; \boldsymbol{\delta}'\mathbf{X}_{ic}
  \;+\; \varepsilon_{ic},
\end{equation}
where $\text{Treat}_{ic}=\texttt{treated\_all}$ (any subsidy arm),
$\text{HighWTP}_i = \mathbf{1}[\text{WTP-stated}_i > \text{median}]$,
and $\mathbf{X}_{ic}$ includes community controls (county, market
proximity, transformer timing, baseline connection rate, population) and
household controls (gender, age, housing quality, baseline energy
spending). For the pooled D2 specification we include a Round~2 fixed
effect. Column~(2) of Table~\ref{tab:main} reports $\hat{b}_1$ (the
ITT for low-WTP households); column~(3) reports $\hat{b}_1+\hat{b}_3$
(the ITT for high-WTP households); column~(4) reports $\hat{b}_3$ (the
differential). Standard errors are clustered at the community level
(\texttt{siteno}). We apply the Benjamini--Hochberg (1995) procedure to
adjust for multiple testing.

% ── 3. Results ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

Figure~\ref{fig:coefplot} plots the ITT coefficients for low-WTP (hollow
circles) and high-WTP (filled circles) households, with 95\,\% confidence
intervals. For both D2 (life satisfaction) and D6 (security index, R2
only), the two groups' estimates are close together and straddle zero with
overlapping confidence intervals. There is no visual evidence of a positive
expectations effect.

Table~\ref{tab:main} presents the full regression results in the format
of Lee, Miguel \& Wolfram (2019) Table~3. For D2 (Panel~D, row~D2),
the low-WTP ITT is $0.094^{**}$ ($p=0.042$), consistent with the
positive average effect in the original paper. The high-WTP ITT is
$0.076$ ($p=0.127$), and the differential $\hat{b}_3=-0.018$
(SE$=0.066$, $p=0.787$). After Benjamini--Hochberg correction the FDR
$q$-value is $0.950$, far from conventional significance thresholds.

For D6 (Panel~D, row~D6), neither group's ITT is significant, and the
differential is $-0.012$ ($p=0.893$, $q=0.950$). Panel~E reports the
mean treatment effect on the noneconomic index (average of standardised
D2 and D6 z-scores): the differential is $-0.047$ ($p=0.486$),
again insignificant. Across all rounds and with or without controls,
point estimates of $b_3$ range from $-0.071$ (R2, D2) to $+0.032$
(R1, D2). The robustness specification using continuous WTP
(WTP-stated in USD) yields $\hat{b}_3=0.0001$ ($p=0.647$).

Across all three rounds and both with and without controls, the point

% ── 4. Caveats ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Caveats}
\label{sec:caveats}

\paragraph{WTP measure is binary and partially random.}
Because each household sees a single randomly assigned elicitation
price, we cannot recover a complete demand curve at the individual
level. The \texttt{wtp\_high} indicator conflates the household's
underlying valuation with the random price draw: a household assigned
a high price that accepts may differ systematically from one assigned a
low price that accepts. The median split on WTP-stated is therefore a
coarse proxy for true willingness-to-pay.

\paragraph{Null average effects constrain power.}
The original papers find small average effects on \texttt{life\_index}
(ITT $\approx 0.09$~SD in our pooled sample). With such small signal,
detecting heterogeneity of plausible magnitude would require a
substantially larger sample or stronger variation in WTP.

\paragraph{D6 is exploratory and R2-only.}
The perceptions-of-security index (\texttt{r\_crime\_index}) is not
available in R1 and does not survive FDR correction in the original
paper. Results for D6 should be treated as exploratory only.

\paragraph{Attrition.}
The \texttt{demand.dta}--\texttt{impacts\_2.dta} merge (2014--2017)
has not been validated against the original paper's attrition tables.
The WTP merge rate in the pooled sample is 99.5\,\%, suggesting minimal
non-response, but differential attrition by WTP group cannot be ruled
out without a formal balance check.
